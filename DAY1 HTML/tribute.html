Early Life

Bhagat Singh was born on September 28, 1907, in a Punjabi Sikh family in Banga, Punjab. His father, Kishan Singh, was a member of the Ghadar Party, a revolutionary organization that aimed to overthrow British rule in India.

Bhagat Singh was a bright student and was inspired by the nationalist movements in India. He joined the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) at the age of 16 and became a prominent figure in the organization.

Political Activism

Bhagat Singh was a fierce critic of British colonialism and imperialism. He believed in using revolutionary means to overthrow British rule in India.

In 1928, Bhagat Singh and his comrades, Shivaram Rajguru and Sukhdev Thapar, were involved in the assassination of John Saunders, a British police officer who was responsible for the brutal lathi charge on Lala Lajpat Rai. Bhagat Singh and his associates surrendered after the incident and were sentenced to death.

Imprisonment and Death

Bhagat Singh and his comrades were held in Lahore Central Jail and were subjected to inhumane treatment. Bhagat Singh went on a hunger strike to protest against the mistreatment of political prisoners and the British colonial government.

On March 23, 1931, Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Thapar were hanged to death in Lahore jail. Their martyrdom inspired millions of Indians to join the freedom struggle.

Legacy

Bhagat Singh's sacrifice and dedication to the Indian freedom struggle continue to inspire generations of Indians. He remains an iconic figure in Indian history and is considered a hero of the Indian independence movement.

Songs

Although not written by Singh, the patriotic Hindustani songs, "Sarfaroshi Ki Tamanna" ("The desire to sacrifice") created by Bismil Azimabadi,[133] and "Mera Rang De Basanti Chola" ("O Mother! Dye my robe the colour of spring") created by Ram Prasad Bismil,[134] are largely associated with him and have been used in a number of related films.[135]
